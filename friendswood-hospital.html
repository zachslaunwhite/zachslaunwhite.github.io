Background

The following background information has been provided by the client:

“ Friendswood Hospital is a 10-floor hospital in a medium-sized city. Ms. Zia Oleg, the IT director is responsible for the network at the medical facility and it has been determined that the current network infrastructure is completely inadequate for the needs of the organization, its employees, and its patients. Your team has been tasked with creating a network system design proposal that Ms. Oleg can present to the hospital’s board of directors for their approval.

As the first step of constructing the system design proposal, we need to understand the fundamental business processes that staff will regularly perform on the network. While there are a variety of other business functions such as accounting, payroll, and technical support to name a few, we will focus our attention on the core patient care and tracking aspects of the organization.

The following groups form the main, patient-facing employee groups of the organization:

·         Administrative staff working at desktop computers on the second (main) floor will access the proprietary patient tracking software (PTS) to enter new patients into the data store, control admissions, and schedule appointments.

·         Laboratory technicians on floors 3-5 will use desktop computers to access the PTS to view appointments as well as to store documents, scans, and reports related to the results of patient tests.

·         Nurses working with patients on floors 6-10, where all patient rooms are, will use mobile devices and/or laptops to access the PTS via wireless connections primarily to track the administration of medication and other treatments.

·         Doctors working with patients on floors 6-10, will also utilize the wireless network to access the PTS to maintain patient charts, track patient progress, and specify patient discharge dates.

While the network infrastructure at Friendswood Hospital is wholly outdated and obsolete, and the goal is to replace all the network components, cabling, and computer devices, knowledge of the existing network setup may help to inform the new design.

The first floor (i.e. basement) of the hospital currently contains the server room/data centre, and WAN connection. There are also approximately 25 desktop client machines on this floor to support some Managerial and Support (e.g. HR & Tech Services) personnel.

The second (i.e. main) floor contains about 40 client machines, which mostly supporting hospital Administrative staff with some additional Managerial and Support personnel.

Floors 3-5 contain approximately 20 desktop machines each to support Laboratory technicians and their Administrative personnel.

On each of the patient floors, there are currently only two to three desktop client machines at the nurses’ stations with an additional 4-5 per floor in doctor’s offices.

Friendswood Hospital hopes to provide secure wireless access within patient rooms so doctors and nurses may enter information in the patient tracking software (PTS) on mobile devices without having to take notes and enter data back on their respective desktop machines. However, the desktop machines would remain for use when in the offices or at the nurses’ stations.

The Friendswood Hospital building has external dimensions of 200-by-100 feet, with ten stories as mentioned before. Every floor of the hospital has some common characteristics:

·         Stairwells on all corners

·         Two Elevators in the center of the floor

·         Washrooms in the center of both east and west walls opposite the elevators

·         A wiring closet on the back side of the elevators that is paired with a utility room

* Can assume the wiring closet will have conduits for running cabling between floors

On the first floor (basement) we have:

·         A large, 20-by-30-foot server/data room on the south side of the building in the center of the floor, near the wiring closet

·         Three other slightly smaller utility rooms near the server room for furnace, storage, etc.

·         Approximately 25 cubicles for some Managerial and Support staff

·         On the second floor (main) we have:

·         A large open entrance area along the north wall in the middle which we can assume has space for the cafeteria as well

·         Approximately 40 cubicles/small offices/work areas for mostly Administrative staff with some additional Managerial and Support personnel

On Floors 3-5 we have:

·         Three large laboratory areas, with an administrative office with a single workstation and partitioned room for three technicians with lab spaces and workstations for each (so, 3 x 4 = 12 workstations)

·         Four smaller lab spaces with spaces for two technicians to work and workstations for each (so, 4 x 2 = 8 workstations)

On each of the patient floors, there are:

·         Six 2-room patient rooms along north wall

·         Four single patient rooms along both sides

·         Two Nurses’ stations: each in the middle of both halves of the building with either one or two workstations

·         Five Doctor’s offices each with a single workstation along the south walls, behind an interior wall or partition

As an addendum to the previously mentioned dimensions and architectural details of Friendswood Hospital, it should be noted that the floors are ten feet in height. While, with any dropped ceilings or raised floors on a floor, the ceiling height might vary, each floor is essentially 10 feet above the previous one.

Friendswood Hospital has agreements in place to receive discounts by exclusively purchasing their equipment with both Cisco (10%) for networking devices such as routers, switches, hubs, and access points and Dell (15%) for servers and workstations. All other necessary equipment will be purchased at retail prices and, preferably, locally whenever possible.

Due to cutbacks of a few years’ previous, Friendswood Hospital maintains a minimal Network Administration and Tech Support staff, with only one full-time employee onsite for each shift with off duty staff on-call in case of emergency and some part-time staff added during peak times. Given the overtime rates specified in the union contract and the downtime mandated for each employee before returning to shift work, the management has decided to outsource the network installation tasks to an outside contractor. Once the new network is in place, the existing employees will be tasked with supporting and maintaining it.

The current network at Friendswood Hospital is being replaced because the network usage outgrew its capabilities and resulted in unacceptable availability and response times of the key patient tracking software (PTS). There is no budget to perform such a wholesale replacement of the network for the foreseeable future after your new design is in place. While some additional equipment can be purchased yearly at reasonable costs and some equipment such as workstations will naturally need to be updated to newer models, we must prove to the administration that the designed network will be performing adequately five years from now with no more than minor tweaks and additions no matter what type of reasonably anticipated growth or increased load that occurs on the network.

Friendswood Hospital Management is also very concerned about security, as any hospital would be. There are numerous government regulations, policies, and guidelines that dictate the secure storage, access, and privacy rights of medical information, so we must always be aware of the security of those records. However, we cannot lose track of “standard” network security threats as a Denial of Service attack on a core piece of network equipment that brings down access to critical patient information is potentially even more damaging than unauthorized access to that patient information.

While money has been set aside in the capital budget for the necessary replacement of the computer network that your design proposal addresses, there is absolutely no budget for an increase to current technical support/network administration staff. The current staff must be able to maintain the network once the contractors have installed it and trained them on any new equipment or technologies. ”

(NSCC, 2019)

Network Design Methodology/Topology

A Hierarchical Network Design allows for a scalable, redundant, secure and high-performance network. This type of design is a cost-efficient way of building a network, in terms of achieving a high degree of performance within a reasonable budget. In addition, thanks to hierarchical network design’s modular approach, the network can be easily expanded or managed.

Hierarchical networks are divided into three layers: core, distribution, and access. Each layer can be customized and optimized to meet the specific needs of a network. This is key to how hierarchical networks provide such impressive benefits.

It is our opinion that a Hierarchical Network Design should be used in the creation of Friendswood Hospital’s network. We have determined the PTS system, due to its status as an essential business function, will require a high degree of redundancy and accessibility. Another key concern is security, due to the sensitive data stored in a hospital environment. Hierarchical Network Design excels in these areas, in addition to the other benefits previously mentioned. In conclusion, we highly recommend that Hierarchical Network Design be used when planning the hospital’s network.

For more information on how Hierarchical Network Design will benefit the Friendswood Hospital, please view the High Availability Network Resource section.

(Cisco Networking Academy, 2014) (Wepman, n.d.)

Top Layer (Core)

The core layer will consist of one or more devices that act as the median between the ISP and the hospital’s network. This layer will provide fast routing speeds to the distribution layer. While the above table assumes a three-tier approach, the core layer may be combined with the distribution layer to minimize costs (as per our recommendation in the Collapsed Core Design section).

Middle Layer (Distribution)

At this level, the network is distributed throughout the hospital connecting all 10 floors together. This layer can be set up with multiple routes to maximize redundancy and network speed while allowing for further scalability. This layer will contain L3 Switches and routers.

Bottom Layer (Access)

This layer is where all the end devices connect to the network. This not only includes the physical connection of desktops, but also access points/Wi-Fi extenders for mobile devices to connect to the designed WLAN for nurses and doctors. In terms of switches, this layer should only contain low-level devices intended for forwarding purposes.

(Cisco Networking Academy, 2014)

Collapsed Core Design

A collapsed core design features only two layers - a core/distribution layer and an access layer - within the design hierarchy. The core and distribution layers are combined into a single layer and share functionality. This provides similar benefits to a three-tier approach, with limited reduction in performance and customization, while reducing setup and maintenance cost. For these reasons, Collapsed Core Design is often seen in small enterprises.

We believe that several aspects of the Friendswood Hospital lend itself well to a Collapsed Core Design, including:

·         The size of the hospital (under 200 devices)

·         The hospital resides in a single building

This hospital’s size, from a business perspective, would fit into Cisco’s classification of a small business. It is beneficial to use a Collapsed Core Design for this type of network because it significantly lessens equipment and complexity requirements while still providing the key benefits of Hierarchical Network Design.

These considerations lead us to believe a Collapsed Core Design could be appropriate for Friendswood. However, further investigation, and consultation with the client, may be required to determine this with certainty.

(Cisco Networking Academy, 2014)

Our solution for the network design will implement the Partial Mesh topology to provide the necessary redundancy and availability required by the PTS system. Partial Mesh provides comparable redundancy and high availability, without the complexity and high cost of a Full Mesh topology. Networking devices on the access layer do not necessarily need to be directly interconnected, leaving this task for the more robust devices on the collapsed distribution/core layer.

(Richardson, Mesh Versus Hierarchical Mesh Topologies, 2019) (Richardson, Partial Mesh Topology, 2019)


 

High Availability Network Resources

The hospital’s network requirements for high availability network resources will be met by utilizing Hierarchical Network Design. This design addresses these requirements by providing fault tolerance, redundancy, speed, accessibility, scalability, and security to the network.

This section will give a brief description of how each requirement is met and how it relates to the hospital’s network, including the PTS system.

Fault Tolerance & Redundancy

Superior redundancy can be achieved by utilizing multiple connections in key areas throughout the network design. For example, in a hierarchical network, access layer switches have multiple connections to the distribution layer switches, and distribution layer switches have multiple connections to core layer switches. This provides the hospital with fault tolerance to protect important systems, such as the PTS, against expansive outages.

Accessibility and Speed

Hierarchical networks leverage link aggregation and high-performance switches at the core and distribution layers to provide fast communication. This will ensure the hospital has the necessary performance to provide end users with excellent accessibility while allowing the PTS system to operate efficiently. In addition, implementing a partial-mesh network will provide an additional degree of accessibility and speed.

Scalability

H ierarchical networks are designed with modularity in mind. This means that it’s easy to add on to the network as required. If the hospital was to expand or grow, the hierarchical network design allows the hospital’s network to grow with it.

Security

Port security is used at the access layer to ensure only authorized devices can access the network. At the distribution layer, a variety of policies can be used to control the network’s traffic. Security is an important aspect of the hospital network considering the sensitive data stored within the PTS system. A hierarchical network is an ideal choice to enable the network to achieve a high degree of security with proper configuration.

(Lewis, 2008)

 


 

Other considerations

VLANs

VLANs will be used on the new network for the Friendswood Hospital to provide logical segmentation of devices that belong to a given department. This will allow for ease of management and for the enforcement of security policies.

Based on the information provided, we anticipate the use of at least 6 VLANs for the hospital’s network:

·         VLAN 10: Tech Services - L ocated on the first floor/basement, used by Tech Services staff.

·         VLAN 20: Administrative/HR – Used by a dministrative and management staff mostly on the main floor, but some also on the basement.

·         VLAN 30: Laboratory – S upporting the technicians which work on floors 3 to 5.

·         VLAN 40: Patient Care – N urses and doctors working on the patient floors (floors 6 through 10). This VLAN will support wired devices at the nurse’s stations and doctor’s offices, as well as wireless APs to be used by Patient Care staff.

·         VLAN 50: Public – To be used by patients, visitors and guests without gaining access to the rest of the network and hospital systems. The public wireless network will be made available throughout the building.

·         VLAN 99: Management – U sed by Tech services, but only for network management/maintenance.

Wireless LANs

It is important to provide excellent coverage and performance on the patient floors to facilitate the use of the PTS system. To address this, nurses and doctors will have access to a private Wireless Network (on a dedicated VLAN for Patient care), which will provide them with access to the hospital’s PTS via mobile devices (cell phones, tablets and or/laptops). Several Enterprise access points will be distributed through floors 6 to 10, in order to provide 100% wireless coverage for the staff.

The general public (patients, family, visitors and guests) will have access to a separate Wireless network, on its own VLAN, with a lower bandwidth and very strict set of policies. This will prevent the introduction of malware on the main network, as well as virtually eliminate downtime/slowdowns on the main network, while still providing the public with the necessary resources to connect to the Internet. Fewer Enterprise access points will be installed in key spots throughout the 10 floors of the hospital, for optimal coverage of the areas in which the public has access to. No access to the production network via this Public Wireless network/VLAN will be permitted.

Cisco’s SAFE Security Reference Architecture

Cisco SAFE Architecture provides systems and network administrators with tools that ensure confidentiality, integrity and high availability of data and resources that support key business functions. Friendswood Hospital will benefit by referencing Cisco’s SAFE for Small Networks, as well as the Cisco SAFE Campus Architecture Guide.

As a hospital allows for employees, customers (in this case patients) and third parties (family, visitors and the public in general) to have access to both its geographical location and network – on a physical and logical scale, quite a few practices outlined in SAFE can apply to the network’s design. The beneficial practices include:

·         Securing access to the network.

·         Securing communications, by means of securing email, voice and video communications that could potentially make the network and its systems vulnerable to outside threats.

·         Securing web access, by enforcing usage policy.

Small networks and Campus type architectures are vulnerable to various threats such as phishing, unauthorized network access, malware propagation, web-based exploits, BYOD (bring your own device) attacks, and more. These threats and attacks come from different “surfaces” – humans, devices, networks, and applications. SAFE can implement the following security capabilities to each of these surfaces:

Human

Threats come from users (employees, patients, visitors, guests, etc.). Identity based access can be used as a security measure.

Devices

PCs, laptops, smartphones and tablets. Client based security systems such as anti-malware, anti-virus, cloud security and personal firewalls can be used as pre-emptive measures.

Network

Applies to physical devices, such as routers and switches on wired networks, as well non standardized mobile devices on wireless networks, and the analysis of traffic. Network security capabilities, such as firewalls, intrusion prevention a policy-based segmentation should be applied, as well as wireless rogue detection and intrusion prevention in conjunction with anti-malware, threat intelligence, flow analytics, web security and the use of VPNs.

Applications

Essentially applied to severs. Security software capable of preventing the distribution of malware, viruses, redirection to malicious sites and unauthorized access.

(Cisco Systems, Inc., 2018)  (Cisco Systems, Inc., 2010)

Ongoing Plans

Scalability

Identify

Network scalability can be defined as the potential of a company to meet future demand based on its existing network resources. A network with excellent scalability is highly beneficial as it allows management to quickly address its own growing needs. Scalability also allows a company to adapt and stay competitive in a changing environment by preparing the network for unexpected expansion.

(Technopedia, n.d.)

In the case of the Friendswood Hospital, the necessity for growth is dictated by the number of patients the hospital receives, or expansions of the hospital for business or research purposes – both which lead to increased staff and network requirements. Based on this information, we have identified the following areas where scalability is a core concern:  

·         Wireless Connectivity : Wireless connectivity is of the utmost importance to the PTS system because it allows hospital staff to access patient records on the fly. This must also be balanced with a high demand for guest network access on the lobby and patient floors. For this reason, it is important for wireless connectivity to be scalable.

·         General Network Performance: In a health-care environment, where performance and efficiency are essential to aiding the hospital staff in caring for patients, it is important to ensure network speeds and capabilities are scalable.

·         PTS Software: The PTS software is an essential business function within the network. The storage and network resources must always be able to quickly adapt to growth and changes. In addition, the software itself must be scalable.

·         Network Equipment and Devices : The hospital must have access to network equipment and devices so that the hospital is able to quickly support growth.

In addition, we have identified one barrier to our Scalability planning:

·         Human Resources: With the limited staff of the hospital’s IT Staff, it is important to consider how Friendswood Hospital can maintain scalability without a large staff to support it. Human resources must be a consideration in the above areas of concern.

(Perna, 2013)

Planning

We believe the network was designed for scalability, thanks to our utilization of hierarchical network design. In addition, we believe that the wireless connectivity, and other areas of access, have left significant room for growth in most areas of the buildings. To address the remaining concerns, we have created a list of planning recommendations:

·         Network Equipment and Devices: Purchasing and storing additional equipment will ensure we have the necessary inventory to account for growth or equipment failure. We recommend maintaining an equipment inventory equal to 25% of the equipment currently in use.

·         Explore Cloud Solutions: Considering the limitations of the IT staff, we believe it is important to do preliminary exploration and planning into cloud solutions. We believe cloud solutions, specifically for something like the PTS system, could be a distinct benefit in handling limited human resources while also providing the type of scalability provided by third-party cloud solutions.

·         Continue to Maintain Room for Network Growth: While we currently have ample room for growth in terms of our network infrastructure - including bandwidth, wireless connectivity, and access - this may not always be the case. It is important to ensure that 25% of the network is left available for growth.

·         Track and Test Network Performance: We believe regular testing to gauge how the network performs in various scenarios, including load testing, could be a benefit. This will allow us to keep an up-to-date overview on how scalable the network is.

Security

Here at Friendswood, information security is essential to the day to day hospital operations. This security plan is recognized as a mission critical process and is the responsibility of all Friendswood employees.

With this plan, we intend to:

·         Identify information security risks

·         Plan an appropriate response to impending threats

·         Document and execute planning and procedures

Identify

As the first part of the security plan, we will identify security risks to the network. Security risks will be broken into “physical”, “logical”, and “human” risks:

Physical 

·         Access to hardware

·         Physical room security

·         Logical

User permissions 

·         Vulnerabilities 

·         Lack of Detection or Protection Capabilities

Access concerns

·         Human

·         Social Engineering 

·         User Error

Planning

To address physical risks, we have made the following recommendations:

·         Server Location: Location of servers and network switches and routers are kept on the basement floor, behind secured doors that require swipe cards.

·         Physical Access Control: Only Authorized personnel have permissions for room access. Employees must notify Network administrators prior to any access to room.

·         Physical Security System: Alert system for when door is open. Switches that are in secured wiring closets on each floor that also require a swipe card (no notification will be required; however, an alert system will still be in place). 

To address logical risks, we have made the following recommendations:

·         Remote Connection: Network administrators will have access to servers, switches, and routers remotely as to not have to access the Server room constantly.

·         Credentials: Admin credentials will only be given to authorized administrators, and users will have a range of permissions depending on the work they need to accomplish. 

·         Monitoring: Network activity will be monitored, including successful logins, login attempts, and the system they are trying to access.

·         Alerts: Alert system for every successful login, and for when there are multiple failed login attempts. 

·         Access Control: Access control lists put in place for access from only specific IP addresses.

·         Security Firewall: We will install and configure a firewall to protect the network.
To address human risks, we have made the following recommendations:

·         Software Training: Training will be required on software usage, what information can and can’t be put into the PTS system.

·         Security Training: Training will be mandatory for introductory security knowledge. 

·         Social Engineering Training: Training on Social Engineering will be mandatory.

Maintenance and Management

Identify

The Friendswood Hospital Maintenance and management plan outlines the steps necessary in order to keep the systems and network running, making sure that issues can be solved before they occur. On the other hand, accounting for every possible scenario is virtually impossible. Things can and will go wrong, eventually. 

Assuming that the contractors hired to setup the servers and the networking equipment (physically and logically) will provide documentation for the services/work performed (based on the Hospital’s requirements), the first step for the IT staff is to review said documentation in order to get familiarized with the current state/setup of the system (the physical and logical topologies, inventory of equipment, IP addressing and interfaces used, location for configuration backups, system images, etc.). After the staff has a good overview of the current state, regularly scheduled tasks will be implemented to maintain the system in good working order. 

Planning

Tasks to consider for maintenance/management of the network/system: 

·         Configuration changes:  Changes must be planned and then authorized before implemented. All changes will be logged via change management logs. Before changes are made, quite a few steps need to be determined: who’s responsible for authorizing the changes; what tasks will be performed; what is the maintenance window; who will be affected; procedures before a change is implemented; steps to assess the success/failure of a change; roll back plan in case of failure/unexpected problems; documenting the change; situations in which change policies can be overruled. 

·         Performance:  monitor the performance on all LANs and WANs in order to understand traffic statistics and bandwidth usage in order to identify possible bottlenecks on the current config. 

·         Faults:  configure network devices to capture log messages in the case something goes wrong or is not working the way it should, such as sudden changes on an interface status (either goes down or comes up when it shouldn’t). 

·         Equipment failure:  Faulty production hardware that cannot be fixed on site must be replaced immediately (25% more hardware then the actual number used on the production network was purchased to have immediate replacement units).

·         Security:  create policies and implement them by using a firewall, ACLs, monitoring systems and authentication servers (RADIUS). 

·         Changes in the business structure:  make sure changes can be made to accommodate a dynamic business environment (i.e. staff can change positions/departments and changes to ACLs and policies might need adjustment).

·         System updates:  maintain an up-to-date system. Network devices and end point devices should not be running on outdated versions (possible security vulnerability). Update documentation to be verified and updates to be deployed and tested on a sandbox environment before been applied on production environment. Schedule updates to be applied for off-hours or for when they will have the least impact on the system/network/device. If a department needs all equipment up and running 24/7, schedule devices to be updated on different time slots, so staff uninterrupted has access to at least 1 available resource/device. 

·         Backups and Recovery:  schedule backups of network equipment configuration, such as access points, switches and router current running configs, in order to deploy replacement equipment as fast as possible. Daily backups of the PTS and file server are to be made. Patient information must be saved on two different locations. Servers are setup for redundancy. Maintain up-to-date repository of system images in case end devices need to be replaced. Determine windows in time in which the backups will have the least impact on the system. Check and test backups constantly.

·         Prioritization:  Create a ranking system in order to classify the severity of situations and the time frame in which they must be dealt with (i.e. replacing a faulty switch takes precedence over replacing a workstation, but replacing a workstation takes precedence over replacing the printer tonner) 

·         Quality of service:  prioritize services/bandwidth for the production network. Configure QoS on the appropriate interfaces. Limit bandwidth on the guest network.

·         Company policies and guidelines:  staff must abide by the policies in place and refer to guidelines whenever necessary. 

·         Staff training/conduct:  Provide training sessions on how to work with the system/network. What to expect of the resources made available to the departments. Proper care/handling of equipment. Regular maintenance tasks/troubleshooting procedures (i.e. Does the printer have paper? Is the power bar on? Is your user connected to the system?) Provide clear information about how to contact the IT department (emails, extension numbers, schedule, office location, etc.)

(Networklessons.com, n.d.)

Management/Maintenance Schedule

·         Daily:  verify backups and replication; verify servers’ storage utilization X capacity; check logs (system, network and security); check ticketing system for user requests; check AD replication; check for account lockouts/unusual user activities.

·         Weekly: verify RAID array on the servers; analyze network traffic patterns and bandwidth usage; review server resource usage; check for critical system updates; test backups; verify backup of config files/system images are up-to-date; physically inspect devices on the datacenter; check for hardware errors; test remote connectivity on servers/network devices; review server maintenance procedures; review security updates and install if needed (approve in WSUS).

·         Monthly:  do a monthly reboot of servers/network equipment; check battery usage on the UPSs; review inactive/locked out/disable/expired user accounts; check firmware updates on switches, routers, firewall, and server’s BIOS.

(Dobran, 2019)  (Lutton, 2019)
